Prominent Asian Americans and Pacific Islanders with connections to Maryland

By Ela Jalil






Members of the Asian American Pacific Islander community in Maryland have broken barriers across the state and the country.


Whether it’s being the first in some of the highest positions in state politics, to creating a rapidly growing restaurant chain that infuses Asian culture into its dishes, this May celebrates the accomplishments of the AAPI community.


In 1992, Congress passed legislation which permanently designated every May to be Asian/Pacific American Heritage Month. Since then, AAPI Heritage Month has been a period of time to celebrate the accomplishments of AAPI individuals and remember the rich history of the community in the United States.


Before looking at current trailblazing members of the community, it is important to understand two historical figures, professor at the University of Maryland and director of the Asian American studies program Janelle Wong said.

““It's hard to understand these other famous people who've come through or prominent Asian Americans without…also thinking about that.” ”

Pyon Su was the first Korean student to attend university in America, and graduated from the Maryland Agricultural College in 1891. He previously served as a Korean ambassador before being forced to flee the country during political turmoil, according to a UMD archives blog post.

In 1915, Chunjen Constant Chen was the first Chinese student to enroll at the Maryland Agricultural College, which is now the University of Maryland. He later received his masters at the college, and returned to UMD and was eventually named a professor emeritus.

Both individuals represent the duality of stereotypes that Asian Americans face - the forever foreigner and the model minority, Wong said.

Susan's words
“I’m very proud to be Asian American, but I’m proud to be an American too. I think I have the best of both worlds, but, I think we bring a different perspective, particularly to the political table. We not only have our perspective from a newcomer or an immigrant group, but we also have the perspective from a woman, a person of color, but also the area that we represent, we’re in tune with those issues too, so you’re getting like 3 or 4 in one person. The diverse perspective makes for better laws because they are more inclusive and they’re more effective and they are more real.”
