Prominent Asian Americans with connections to Maryland

By Ela Jalil






Members of the Asian American Pacific Islander community in Maryland have broken barriers across the state and the country.


Whether it’s being the first in some of the highest positions in state politics, to creating a rapidly expanding restaurant that infuses Asian culture into its dishes, this May celebrates the accomplishments of the AAPI community.


In 1992, Congress passed legislation which permanently designated every May to be Asian/Pacific American Heritage Month. Since then, AAPI Heritage Month has been a period of time to celebrate the accomplishments of AAPI individuals and remember the rich history of the community in the United States.


Before looking at current trailblazing members of the community, it is important to understand two historical figures, professor at the University of Maryland and director of the Asian American studies program Janelle Wong said.

“It's hard to understand these other famous people who've come through or prominent Asian Americans without…also thinking about that.”

— Professor Janelle Wong

Pyon Su was the first Korean student to attend university in America, and graduated from the Maryland Agricultural College in 1891. He previously served as a Korean ambassador before being forced to flee the country during political turmoil, according to a UMD archives blog post.

In 1915, Chunjen Constant Chen was the first Chinese student to enroll at the Maryland Agricultural College, which is now the University of Maryland. He later received his masters at the college, and returned to UMD and was eventually named a professor emeritus.

Both individuals represent the duality of stereotypes that Asian Americans face - the forever foreigner and the model minority, Wong said.

Susan Lee

Career highlights
First Asian American secretary of state of Maryland
Ethnicity
Chinese American

Susan Lee has worn many hats throughout her career.


Born in Texas to a Chinese immigrant mother and a father of Chinese descent, Susan Lee moved to Maryland at a young age. She grew up in Montgomery County and later attended the University of Maryland and law school in San Francisco.


In California she found a strong Asian American community, but felt pulled back to Maryland because of its proximity to the nation’s capital. In 1998 she helped create the Coalition of Asian Pacific American Democrats of Maryland, which works to empower the AAPI community’s voice within the Democratic Party, Lee said.


She served over 20 years between the Maryland House of Delegates and the Maryland Senate, starting her first time in office in 2002. During her time in the legislature, she passed bills ranging from gun safety and fighting domestic violence to cyber security and consumer protection. While in the senate, she was the lead sponsor on a law to ban ghost guns.


In 2023, Gov. Wes Moore appointed Lee as Maryland's 72nd secretary of state. She is the first Asian American to hold that position, and previously broke barriers as the first Asian American elected to the Maryland Senate, according to the Maryland State Archives.


“I'm very honored to be the first, but I don't want to be the last, and I hope that the foundation that I've laid, that I continue to work to lay, will help our future generations,” Lee said. “Because without these future generations, we don't have a future.”

More words from Lee

“I’m very proud to be Asian American, but I’m proud to be an American too. I think I have the best of both worlds, but, I think we bring a different perspective, particularly to the political table.


We not only have our perspective from a newcomer or an immigrant group, but we also have the perspective from a woman, a person of color, but also the area that we represent, we’re in tune with those issues too, so you’re getting like 3 or 4 in one person.


The diverse perspective makes for better laws because they are more inclusive and they’re more effective and they are more real.”

Steve Chu

Career highlights
Co-founder of Ekiben
Ethnicity
Taiwanese American

Although Steve Chu grew up surrounded by family members in the restaurant industry, he wasn’t initially sure he wanted to become a chef.


It wasn’t until he got to college, at the University of Maryland, Baltimore County, that he realized that the restaurant business could become a creative outlet for him. He started Ekiben as a hot dog cart at a farmers market and just wanted to have fun with food. The restaurant now has four store front locations, one of which just opened in late April.


He and the other founders were able to combine their cultural influences with their experiences growing up in America into their food and business practices.


Describing himself as a “Maryland kid” Chu found an authenticity and pride in Maryland to base his business in that was hard to find anywhere else, he said. Known for their unique flavors and giant bao bun sandwiches, Ekiben has become a staple spot in Baltimore.


Chu was a semi-finalist in 2023 for a James Beard award, but you wouldn’t know that walking into his restaurant. It’s all a part of his goal to make sure Ekiben isn’t taking itself too seriously.


“There are always expectations and stereotypes when it comes to Asian food, but Ekiben’s been a really good platform to use to help people expand what they think is possible with Asian food in America.”

More words from Chu

“I think that's something that many Asian Americans have in common is we're extremely hard workers because we just see how hard our parents have struggled just to survive here.


I think for me being Asian American, [the] biggest strength is being able to see the perseverance of our parents and being able to inherit that. And taking that strength and using that to push forward into our own hopes and dreams.”

Aruna Miller

Career highlights
First Asian American Liutenant Governor of Maryland
Ethnicity
Indian American

Aruna Miller likes to say she came to the East Coast for love.


She immigrated to the United States when she was seven-years-old from India, and primarily grew up in Missouri. There she became a civil engineer and also met her college sweetheart. She moved with him to Montgomery County, and became a transportation engineer for the county’s department of transportation.


She remembers crying after voting in her first presidential election after becoming a naturalized citizen. Her candidate ultimately lost, which pushed her to become engaged with her local democratic party.


When a delegate seat opened up in her district she initially turned down requests to run saying she was an engineer not a politician. When reminded of her love of public service, she ran and won.


When Moore asked her to be on his ticket, Miller was told by some people that it went against conventional wisdom to put two people of color on the same campaign. The team won 64.7% of the vote and became the first Black and brown ticket to win in U.S. history. As lieutenant governor, she chairs the Interfaith council which brings faith leaders together across all religions.


“I spent most of my life trying to walk into spaces that weren't created with me in mind,” Miller said. “That includes being an immigrant growing up in the Western world, being an Indian-American legislator in a legislature that looked nothing like me, being an engineer, a female engineer in a male-dominated field. But over the years, I realized that the idea is to walk into these spaces, and be exactly who you are.”

More words from Miller

“I'm really proud to represent the unique ability to be an Asian. When I bring my culture and how I was raised into the greater community of Marylanders or being an American or a lieutenant governor I think that makes what I have to offer so much different and unique from others perhaps.”


…There's so many Asians that live in our state, but each one of us comes with our own unique journeys and when we bring that to the table, I think that creates a stronger democracy and a stronger state being able to address the multiple diverse needs of Marylanders.”

Tae Strain

Career highlights
Ethnicity
Korean American

For Chef Tae Strain, cooking has been a key way to become closer to his culture.

The Howard County-raised chef cooked part-time during school, but was drawn to a full-time career in the food industry due to the dynamic energy of the kitchen. He’s cooked in high-profile kitchens in New York and California, but always knew that he would find his way back to Maryland.

He worked as the executive chef for Momofuku’s D.C location from 2017 to 2019, changing up the menu and bringing acclaim to the restaurant, a 2020 Eater article said. After leaving Momofuku, Strain set out to create a project that was entirely his own. He created a pop-up and supper club called Ggoma, or little one in Korean.

The name was a nod to background as a Korean-American adoptee, and also a way to represent the small dinners he was serving. Along with Ggoma, he’s also the culinary director at Westminster Winery in Carroll County.

“We focus 100% on sourcing locally and from the farm, but all the flavors are definitively Asian American. And this is a big thing for me just to be standing on it in that way,” Strain said. “And, It's not that it's definitively Korean at all but I think that it's more representative of my lived experience as an adopted Korean.”

Words

“I think what it means is to be equally proud of both places. The majority of my life, I would have identified as American Asian, simply from a comfort level and what it is to assimilate and feeling more comfortable around white friends. And I think how I view it now is that I identify firmly as Asian American. Being proud of both sides.”

More AAPI Marylanders to follow

AAPI individuals are breaking barriers across all industries. Here's five more to look out for this month.

Rei Ami

Rei Ami, or Sarah Yeeun Lee, is a South Korean born and Montgomery County raised singer and actress. She is most known for her role as Zooey in the Oscar-winning KPop Demon Hunters, which was also the most streamed Netflix movie ever.

Utkarsh Ambudkar

Utkarsh Ambudkar is a Baltimore-born and Montgomery County raised actor, rapper and singer known for roles in Pitch Perfect, Ghosts and Never Have I Ever. He recently released a new album titled Too Beautiful

Bailey Anne Kennedy

In 2024, Bailey Anne Kennedy became the first transgender woman to win Miss Maryland USA, as well as the first Asian American to win. The Cambodian immigrant grew up in Montgomery County and also serves as a volunteer for the United Service Organization.

Taulia Tagovailoa

Taulia Tagovailoa is a professional football player, and former University of Maryland quarterback. In 2023, he was honored with the 2023 Polynesian College Football Offensive Player of the Year award.

Connie Chung

Connie Chung is a Montgomery County raised and University of Maryland graduate who was the first Asian American to anchor an evening news program.
